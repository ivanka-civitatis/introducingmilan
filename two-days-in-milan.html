Two Days in Milan

The definitive 48-hour itinerary — what to see, when to go and what to book in advance

Two days is enough to see Milan's unmissable highlights — the Duomo, the Last Supper, Castello Sforzesco, the Pinacoteca di Brera and the Navigli — without feeling rushed. The key is to book the Last Supper well in advance and organise the route to avoid unnecessary backtracking.

✍️ By Ivanka Garcia Mancebo·Updated June 2026
⚠️
Book the Last Supper before anything else. The Cenacolo Vinciano admits only 30 visitors every 15 minutes and tickets sell out weeks — sometimes months — in advance. Book as soon as you know your travel dates, then plan everything else around your allocated time slot.
1
Historic centre — Duomo, Galleria, La Scala & Brera
9:00 am
Milan Cathedral (Duomo)
Start early to beat the crowds. Visit the cathedral interior (free) first, then take the elevator or stairs to the rooftop for the panoramic views. Allow 1.5–2 hours for both.
→ Book rooftop + guided tour in advance to skip the entrance queue
11:00 am
Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II
Walk through the spectacular 19th-century arcade — free to enter. Find the mosaic bull on the floor near the central octagon and stand on it for good luck (a Milanese tradition). 20–30 minutes.
11:30 am
Piazza della Scala & Teatro alla Scala
The world's most famous opera house. Visit the museum inside (open daily, €9) to see costumes, instruments and a rare view into the auditorium. 45 minutes.
1:00 pm
Lunch in Brera
Head to the Brera neighbourhood (10-minute walk). Lunch at a neighbourhood trattoria on the side streets — avoid the main tourist piazza itself for better value.
2:30 pm
Pinacoteca di Brera
One of Italy's finest art galleries — Raphael's Marriage of the Virgin, Mantegna's Lamentation of Christ, Hayez's The Kiss. Allow 2 hours minimum; 3 with a guided tour.
→ Guided tour strongly recommended — the collection requires context
6:30 pm
Aperitivo in Brera or Navigli
Milan's evening ritual — Campari Spritz or Negroni with complimentary snacks. Brera is more elegant; Navigli is livelier. Both are excellent. This evening, stay in Brera after the gallery visit.

2
The Last Supper, Castello, Navigli & more
Your slot
The Last Supper (Cenacolo Vinciano)
Leonardo da Vinci's masterpiece in the refectory of Santa Maria delle Grazie. Each group has exactly 15 minutes inside — unhurried beforehand, extraordinary inside. The church exterior and Bramante's Sacristy are free.
→ Book well in advance — tickets regularly sell out 2–3 months ahead
After visit
Castello Sforzesco
The great 15th-century Sforza castle — 10 minutes' walk from Santa Maria delle Grazie. The courtyard is free; the 7 museums inside (including Michelangelo's unfinished Rondanini Pietà) require a ticket. Allow 1.5–2 hours.
1:00 pm
Parco Sempione
Milan's largest central park, behind the castle. A pleasant place to sit and rest before the afternoon. The Arco della Pace at the far end is worth a short detour.
2:30 pm
San Bernardino alle Ossa or Pinacoteca Ambrosiana
Two excellent options for the afternoon depending on your interests: the extraordinary ossuary chapel of San Bernardino (free, 15 min) for the curious, or the Pinacoteca Ambrosiana (€17, 2h) for Leonardo's Codex Atlanticus and Caravaggio.
6:00 pm
Navigli for aperitivo & dinner
Milan's canal district — the most atmospheric area for an evening out. Aperitivo from 6pm, dinner from 8pm. Walk along the Naviglio Grande and choose any of the restaurants along the water.

Frequently asked questions

What should I do in Milan in 2 days?+

Day 1: Duomo, Galleria, La Scala museum, Pinacoteca di Brera, aperitivo. Day 2: The Last Supper (pre-booked), Castello Sforzesco, Parco Sempione, Pinacoteca Ambrosiana, Navigli for dinner.

Do I need to book the Last Supper in advance?+

Yes — booking in advance is essential. Tickets sell out weeks or months ahead. Book as soon as you know your travel dates. Only 30 visitors every 15 minutes are allowed inside.

Is 2 days enough to see Milan?+

Two days is enough for the main highlights. With more time you can visit more museums, explore quieter neighbourhoods like Isola and Porta Venezia, and take a day trip to Lake Como or Bergamo.
