Top Attractions in Milan

Monuments, museums, churches, parks and day trips — a complete guide to what to see

✍️ By Ivanka Garcia Mancebo·Updated June 2026

Milan rewards exploration at every level. On the surface it is a city of extraordinary monuments — the Gothic Duomo, the world's most famous opera house, Leonardo da Vinci's Last Supper. But it is also a city of quiet discoveries: a medieval square hidden two minutes from the Duomo, a free gallery with Van Gogh and Cézanne, a 13th-century ossuary chapel that most visitors walk straight past. This guide covers both.

Monuments & sights

Milan's most visited monuments are concentrated in a compact historic centre that can largely be explored on foot. The Duomo is the natural starting point; from there, La Scala, the Galleria and Piazza Mercanti are all within a few hundred metres. The Castello Sforzesco and the Last Supper are in the western part of the centre — logically combined in a single half-day.

Museums

Milan has an exceptional concentration of museums for a city of its size. The Pinacoteca di Brera is one of Italy's finest art galleries; the Ambrosiana holds Leonardo's Codex Atlanticus; and the Galleria d'Arte Moderna gives you Van Gogh and Cézanne free of charge in an 18th-century villa. Churches worth visiting

Milan's churches are among its least-visited and most rewarding sights. San Maurizio al Monastero Maggiore — often called "the Sistine Chapel of Milan" — has extraordinary Luini frescoes and is free. Sant'Ambrogio, the city's patron saint's church, dates to 387 AD and contains his crypt. San Bernardino alle Ossa is one of the most unusual sights in Italy: an ossuary chapel decorated entirely with human bones, steps from the Duomo.

Frequently asked questions

What are the must-see attractions in Milan?+

The absolute must-sees are: the Milan Cathedral (Duomo) and its rooftop, the Last Supper (book well in advance), the Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II, Castello Sforzesco and the Pinacoteca di Brera. For a day trip, Lake Como is unmissable. See our Top 10 guide for the full ranked list.

How many days do you need to see Milan?+

Two days is enough for the main highlights. With three or four days you can see the lesser-known gems (San Maurizio, Piazza Mercanti, the Galleria d'Arte Moderna) and take a day trip to Lake Como or Bergamo. See our 48-hour itinerary.

What is free to see in Milan?+

Many of Milan's best sights are free: the Duomo interior, the Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II, Piazza Mercanti, Parco Sempione, the Cimitero Monumentale, the Galleria d'Arte Moderna (GAM), San Maurizio al Monastero Maggiore, San Bernardino alle Ossa, Basilica di Sant'Ambrogio and the Castello Sforzesco courtyard. The first Sunday of the month is free entry at many civic museums.
