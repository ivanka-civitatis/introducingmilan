Lake Maggiore

Isola Bella, the Borromean Islands & Santa Caterina del Sasso — 80km from Milan

Lake Maggiore (Lago Maggiore, also known as Lake Verbano) is Italy's second largest lake — a long, narrow expanse stretching from Lombardy into Piedmont and Switzerland, with a shoreline shaped by centuries of Borromeo family patronage. Its Baroque islands, cliff-hanging hermitage and elegant lakeside towns make it one of the most romantic and culturally rich of the northern lakes.

✍️ By Ivanka Garcia Mancebo·Updated June 2026·⏱️ 4 min read

What to see

🏝️ Isola Bella
The most famous of the Borromean Islands — an extraordinary 17th-century Baroque palace and terraced garden built on a tiny island by the Borromeo family. One of the most opulent and theatrical sights in northern Italy. Accessible by ferry from Stresa.
🦚 Isola Madre
The largest of the Borromean Islands, with a stunning palace surrounded by a tropical botanical garden where white peacocks roam freely. Quieter and less visited than Isola Bella.
⛪ Santa Caterina del Sasso
A medieval hermitage built into the rock face above the lake near Leggiuno — reachable only by boat or a long staircase. One of the most dramatic and unexpected sights on any of the Italian lakes.
🚡 Laveno & Sasso del Ferro
Laveno has the only natural harbour on Lake Maggiore and a funicular to Sasso del Ferro (1,062m) — spectacular views of the Alps and the lake from the top.
🏖️ Stresa
The main tourist hub on the western shore — a handsome lakeside town with grand hotels, a long promenade and the ferry dock for the Borromean Islands. The best base for a day trip.
🏰 Angera
An imposing medieval fortress (Rocca Borromea) overlooking the lake from the Lombardy shore, with remarkable 14th-century frescoes and a Doll Museum inside.
🏛️ The Borromeo family

The Borromeo family has owned the islands and much of the western shore of Lake Maggiore since the 15th century. Their wealth — made through banking and trade — funded the extraordinary gardens and palaces that make the lake famous. The same family produced Saint Charles Borromeo, Archbishop of Milan and patron saint of the city.

Visiting tips

  • Start in Stresa — it is the most convenient hub, with ferry connections to Isola Bella and Isola Madre departing from the town waterfront.
  • Allow at least 1.5 hours on Isola Bella — the palace and gardens are extensive and deserve time.
  • The hermitage of Santa Caterina del Sasso can be reached by ferry from Stresa or by descending the stairs from the road above Leggiuno — book boat tours in advance in summer.
  • Combine Isola Bella and Isola Madre in a single visit — a combined ferry ticket is available from Stresa.
  • Lake Maggiore extends into Switzerland to the north — if you have extra time, the Swiss town of Locarno is a beautiful extension of the day.

Frequently asked questions

How do I get from Milan to Lake Maggiore?+

By car: A8 motorway then follow signs to Lago Maggiore — 80km, ~1 hour. By train: Cadorna to Laveno (~1.5h, €8); or Porta Garibaldi to Stresa/Arona. By guided tour: day trips from Milan typically include ferry access to the Borromean Islands.

What are the Borromean Islands?+

The Borromean Islands are a group of islands owned by the Borromeo family since the 15th century. Isola Bella has an extraordinary Baroque palace and terraced gardens. Isola Madre is the largest, with a tropical garden and white peacocks roaming freely.

What is the hermitage of Santa Caterina del Sasso?+

A medieval monastery built directly into the rock face above the lake near Leggiuno — one of the most dramatic sights on any of Italy's northern lakes. Reachable by boat from Stresa or by descending the stairs from the road above.

How far is Lake Maggiore from Milan?+

Lake Maggiore is approximately 80km northwest of Milan — about 1 hour by car or 1.5 hours by train from Cadorna station to Laveno.
