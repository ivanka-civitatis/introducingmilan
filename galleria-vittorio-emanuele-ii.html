Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II

✓ Free entry

Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II, affectionately nicknamed "il salotto di Milano" (Milan's drawing room), is an elegant 19th-century shopping arcade and the oldest shopping centre in Italy. Located between the Duomo and Teatro alla Scala, it is one of Milan's most iconic landmarks — and entry is completely free.

✍️ By Ivanka Garcia Mancebo · Updated June 2026 · ⏱️ 4 min read

A bit of history

Constructed between 1865 and 1877 by renowned architect Giuseppe Mengoni, Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II is named after the first king of the Kingdom of Italy. This stunning arcade, with its iconic glass dome, quickly became one of Milan's most beloved meeting places.

Surprisingly, the Galleria wasn't embraced right away. Locals initially opposed the project because a historic part of the city had to be demolished to make room for it. At the time, Italy was going through a difficult unification period, and Mengoni saw the Galleria as a way to bring people together. Its mosaics are filled with patriotic symbols meant to inspire unity.

Tucked between two other must-sees — il Duomo and Teatro alla Scala — the Galleria is a classic example of 19th-century arcade design. It even inspired Naples' Galleria Umberto I, completed about ten years later.

Things to do inside the Galleria

At Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II you'll find luxury brand shops like Prada, Gucci and Louis Vuitton, alongside art galleries, souvenir stores, and even the oldest bookshop in Italy, Libreria Bocca, which has been here since 1775.

Beyond shopping, the Galleria is also a foodie destination. Just steps from the designer stores, you'll find Michelin-starred restaurants and historic cafés. One standout is Café Biffi, one of the oldest restaurants in Milan, serving guests since the 19th century.

Strolling down the grand passageway, don't forget to look up. The central dome features a stunning mosaic representing the continents of Asia, Africa, Europe, and America.

🐂 Local legend

Under the central dome, there's a mosaic featuring a bull representing Turin. Legend says that if you place your right heel on the bull and spin around with your eyes closed, you'll get good luck. Do it at midnight on New Year's Eve and you'll be lucky for the whole year!

Best time to visit

The Galleria is open 24 hours a day, but early morning (before 9am) is the best time to visit for photographs — the light through the glass dome is stunning and there are virtually no crowds. In the evening, the warm lighting gives the arcade a particularly magical atmosphere.

December is spectacular: the Galleria is decorated with an enormous Christmas tree and festive lights, and the New Year's Eve bull-spinning tradition draws thousands of people to the central dome at midnight. If you prefer to avoid the crowds, late January and February are the quietest months.

Where to eat and drink inside the Galleria

The Galleria is also a serious foodie destination. Caffè Biffi, founded in 1867, is one of the oldest restaurants in Milan — perfect for a coffee or aperitivo while watching the world go by. Savini, open since 1867, is the Galleria's most celebrated restaurant, with a terrace directly under the dome; expect to pay around €60–80 per person for a full meal.

For something more casual, Luini — just outside the Galleria on Via Santa Radegonda — is a legendary Milanese institution serving panzerotti (fried stuffed pastry) for under €3. Queues form every day, but move quickly.

If you're here for aperitivo hour (6–9pm), the bars inside and just outside the Galleria are among the most atmospheric spots in the city — though prices reflect the location.

A global inspiration

The Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II didn't just inspire Naples' Galleria Umberto I — its design influenced covered arcades across the world, from the GUM department store in Moscow to the Cleveland Arcade in Ohio. It is widely considered the prototype of the modern shopping mall, making it one of the most architecturally influential buildings of the 19th century.

Visiting tips

  • Entry is completely free — no ticket needed at any time of day or year.
  • Visit early in the morning or in the evening for the best photos without crowds.
  • The Galleria is at its most magical at Christmas, when it's beautifully decorated.
  • The mosaic floors are slippery — wear comfortable shoes with grip.
  • Combine with a visit to the nearby Duomo rooftop for the perfect morning in central Milan.

Frequently asked questions

Is Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II free to visit? +

Yes, entry to Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II is completely free. The arcade is open to the public every day, all year round, 24 hours a day.

What are the opening hours of Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II? +

The Galleria itself is open 24 hours a day, every day of the year. Individual shops and restaurants have their own opening hours.

How do I get to Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II? +

Take the Milan Metro to Duomo station (lines 1 and 3). The Galleria is directly accessible from Piazza del Duomo, right next to the Cathedral.

What is the lucky bull tradition at the Galleria? +

Under the central dome there is a mosaic bull representing Turin. Legend says that if you place your right heel on the bull and spin around with your eyes closed, you'll have good luck. At midnight on New Year's Eve, this is said to bring good luck for the whole year.
