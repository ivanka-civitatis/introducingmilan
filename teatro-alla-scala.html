Teatro alla Scala

La Scala, or Teatro alla Scala in full, is one of the most famous opera houses in the world. Its sober and elegant exterior never fails to surprise those who visit it for the first time — the inside is far more grand than the façade suggests.

✍️ By Ivanka Garcia Mancebo · Updated June 2026 · ⏱️ 5 min read

History of Teatro alla Scala

The Archduke Ferdinand of Austria-Este commissioned the construction of a new Ducal Theatre after fire destroyed the previous building in 1776. The new opera house was built on the site of the former Church of Santa Maria alla Scala — hence the name. It was inaugurated in 1778 with a performance of Europa Riconosciuta by Antonio Salieri.

Like other theatres of the same period, La Scala also housed a casino during its early years. In 1943, during World War II, the theatre was badly damaged by bombing and was reconstructed three years later. In 2002, it closed for two years of renovation and reopened in November 2004 — fittingly, with the same Salieri opera that had inaugurated it 226 years earlier.

World premieres and famous composers

Many of the world's most celebrated operas had their world premiere at La Scala, including Othello and Nabucco by Verdi, and Madame Butterfly by Giacomo Puccini. The theatre has been central to the development of Italian opera for nearly 250 years.

Interestingly, the composer Giuseppe Verdi initially refused to have his work performed at La Scala, convinced that the orchestra modified his compositions. He later changed his mind and went on to establish one of the most celebrated relationships in the theatre's history.

The Theatre Museum

The Theatre Museum (Museo Teatrale alla Scala) contains a large collection of paintings, busts, costumes, and objects related to the world of opera and theatre. A visit includes the theatre's grand foyer — an elegant, sparsely decorated hall — and the small box seats covered in red satin, where high society has enjoyed operas and ballets for centuries.

The enormous auditorium is made of wood and covered in red velvet, adorned with golden stuccos. The stage is lit by a huge Bohemian crystal chandelier with 383 bulbs. The tour gives access to parts of La Scala that are otherwise off-limits, including the boxes and backstage areas.

Piazza della Scala

Between the theatre and the Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II lies Piazza della Scala, a lively square with a large statue of Leonardo da Vinci at its centre. It's a pleasant spot to rest after visiting the theatre, surrounded by greenery and the elegant buildings that frame the square.

Attending a performance

If you want to experience La Scala at its finest, attending a live opera or ballet performance is unmissable. The season runs from 7 December (the feast of Sant'Ambrogio, Milan's patron saint) through to the following November. Tickets range from around €30 for upper gallery seats to several hundred euros for the best boxes. Book well in advance — performances sell out quickly, especially the opening night on 7 December.

For performances, smart elegant attire is expected: jacket and tie for men, and formal dress or smart evening wear for women. Jeans and trainers are not appropriate.

Visiting tips

  • You can only visit the interior by purchasing a museum ticket or a ticket to a performance — there is no free access to the auditorium.
  • Book the guided tour in advance during peak season (June–September) as groups are limited in size.
  • The museum shop sells excellent opera-related gifts and recordings — worth a visit even without a tour.
  • Combine with a stroll through the nearby Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II and a visit to the Duomo — all within 5 minutes' walk.

Frequently asked questions

How can I visit Teatro alla Scala?+

You can visit either by purchasing tickets to a live opera or ballet performance, or by visiting the Theatre Museum, open daily 9:30am–5:30pm. The museum gives access to the auditorium, boxes, foyer and a remarkable collection of opera-related artefacts.

How much does it cost to visit La Scala museum?+

Museum tickets cost €15 for adults, €8 for young people (over 6) and over 65s, and free for children under 6. A family ticket (2 adults + 1 or 2 children aged 6–14) costs €25.

When is Teatro alla Scala closed?+

The Theatre Museum is closed on 1st January, Easter Sunday, 1st May, 15th August, and 7th, 25th and 26th December. Note that 7 December is the opera season opening night — the museum is closed but the theatre itself is very active.

How do I get to Teatro alla Scala?+

Take the Milan Metro to Duomo station (lines 1 and 3) — the theatre is a 2-minute walk. Tram lines 1 and 2 stop at Manzoni Scala, right outside the building.

What is the dress code for La Scala?+

For opera and ballet performances, smart elegant attire is expected: jacket and tie for men, formal or smart evening wear for women. For the Theatre Museum visit during the day, there is no strict dress code.
