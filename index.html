Milan surprises almost every visitor. Most arrive expecting fashion and commerce and leave having discovered something far richer: a city of 2,600 years of history compressed into a walkable centre, where a Roman column stands next to a Renaissance church, where the world's most important painting hangs in a former refectory, and where one of the great Gothic cathedrals of the world has been under continuous construction since 1386.

The Italian Lakes are part of this story too. Lake Como, Lake Maggiore and Lake Garda are not peripheral add-ons to a Milan trip — for many visitors, they are the reason to come. Milan's position at the convergence of three great lake valleys makes it the finest base in Europe for combining a major city with extraordinary natural scenery. This guide covers all of it.

Where to start

Five experiences that define a Milan visit — chosen for what they reveal about the city. See the full Top 10 →

Start here
The Duomo rooftop — Milan's best viewpoint
The interior is free, but the rooftop changes how you see the city. On a clear day you can see the Alps. Book online — the walk-in queue can be 45 minutes. Allow 2 hours for both.
Book before anything else
The Last Supper
15 minutes, 30 people, complete silence. The most intense art experience in Milan — and the one most visitors fail to book in time. Sells out 2–3 months ahead.
The underrated masterpiece
Pinacoteca di Brera
One of Italy's finest art galleries, far less crowded than the Uffizi. Raphael, Mantegna, Caravaggio and Hayez in 38 rooms. Get a guide or you'll miss half of what makes it exceptional.
Afternoon well spent
Castello Sforzesco
The courtyard is free and impressive alone. Inside, Michelangelo's Rondanini Pietà — his final work, unfinished at his death — is one of the most moving sculptures in Italy.
Milan's best-kept secret
Galleria d'Arte Moderna — free
Van Gogh, Cézanne, Picasso and Modigliani in an 18th-century villa. Free, always. Almost never crowded. The most surprising museum in Milan.

Questions visitors ask before arriving

Is Milan worth visiting?+

Yes — Milan is consistently underrated. Beyond fashion and finance, it has the Gothic Duomo, Leonardo's Last Supper, one of Italy's finest art galleries, extraordinary free museums, intact medieval streets, and the best access to the Italian Lakes of any major city. Two days is enough to see the highlights; most visitors want more.

What is Milan most famous for?+

Fashion (Gucci, Prada, Versace, Armani all headquartered here), the Gothic Duomo, Leonardo da Vinci's Last Supper, the Salone del Mobile design fair (the world's most important design event), Teatro alla Scala opera house, and as Italy's financial and business capital.

How do I get from Milan to Lake Como?+

Train from Milano Cadorna to Como Nord Lago — approximately 1 hour, €5.20 one way, every 30 minutes from 7:13am. By car: A9 north, ~50km, 45 minutes. Guided day trips also available. See the Lake Como guide.

What should I book in advance for Milan?+

The Last Supper — book the same day you book your flights. Sells out 2–3 months ahead. The Duomo rooftop — book a few days ahead to skip queues. Hotels — book 3–6 months ahead for Fashion Week (Feb/Sep) and Salone del Mobile (April), when prices triple.

What is the cheapest way from Malpensa Airport to Milan?+

The Terravision or Autostradale bus to Milano Centrale costs €10 and takes ~50 minutes. The Malpensa Express train costs €13 (30 min to Cadorna, 50 min to Centrale). Taxi: €100 fixed price. See the Malpensa guide.

Can you visit Milan in one day?+

Yes — plan it well: Duomo interior (free) + rooftop (pre-booked), Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II, La Scala museum, lunch in Brera, Pinacoteca di Brera in the afternoon, aperitivo in Navigli. The Last Supper requires its own pre-booked half-day. See the 48-hour itinerary.
