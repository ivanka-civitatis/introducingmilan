Milano Centrale Railway Station

Milano Centrale is one of the largest and most spectacular railway stations in Europe — and a genuine architectural landmark worth visiting in its own right. Inaugurated in 1931, its monumental facade and extraordinary interior blend Art Nouveau, Art Deco and Fascist architecture into one of the most dramatic public buildings in Italy.

✍️ By Ivanka Garcia Mancebo · Updated June 2026 · ⏱️ 3 min read

An architectural landmark

Inspired by the modernism of the early 20th century, Milano Centrale is a striking mix of styles — Art Nouveau detailing on the facade blends with the sweeping Art Deco grandeur of the interior, all overlaid with the monumental scale demanded by the Fascist regime under whose watch the station was completed.

History

The station was inaugurated in 1931 to replace the original central station, which had become too small to handle the city's growing rail traffic. The project had begun with a simpler design, but was later transformed into an imposing monumental structure when Mussolini came to power — he wanted the station to represent the grandeur and power of the Fascist regime.

The result is a building of extraordinary scale and ambition. The vast entrance hall, the coffered ceilings, the bronze detailing and the sheer height of the spaces inside make it unlike almost any other station in the world.

What to see inside

Even if you are not catching a train, the station is worth exploring for its architecture alone. Look out for:

  • The monumental facade on Piazza Luigi di Savoia — one of the most impressive in Milan.
  • The enormous glass and steel domes covering the 24 tracks — a feat of early 20th-century engineering.
  • The blue ceramic tiles on the interior walls, each depicting one of the Italian and European cities served by trains from the station.
  • The grand entrance halls with their Art Nouveau and Art Deco decorative details, bronze fittings and coffered ceilings.

Getting to the city centre from Milano Centrale

Milano Centrale is extremely well connected to the rest of the city. The metro station Centrale F.S. is directly beneath the train station and serves both line M2 (green) and line M3 (yellow). The Duomo is just 4 stops on line M3 — approximately 8 minutes. Trams and buses also depart from Piazza Luigi di Savoia and Piazza Duca d'Aosta outside the station.

Frequently asked questions

Is Milano Centrale worth visiting as a tourist attraction?+

Yes — Milano Centrale is widely considered one of the most beautiful railway stations in Europe. The monumental facade, the enormous glass and steel domes, the Art Nouveau and Art Deco detailing, and the blue city tiles inside make it a genuine architectural landmark worth exploring even if you are not catching a train.

When was Milano Centrale built?+

Milano Centrale was inaugurated in 1931, replacing the original central station which had become too small for the city's growing rail traffic. The design was later modified under Mussolini to reflect the grandeur of the Fascist regime.

How do I get from Milano Centrale to the city centre?+

Take the metro from Centrale F.S. station directly beneath the train hall. Line M3 (yellow) reaches the Duomo in 4 stops (approx. 8 minutes). Line M2 (green) connects to Garibaldi, Cadorna and other key points. Trams and buses also depart from the piazzas outside.

How many tracks does Milano Centrale have?+

Milano Centrale has 24 tracks, all covered by the famous glass and steel domes. The station serves over 300,000 passengers every day and connects Milan with major European and Italian cities.
