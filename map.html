Milan's neighbourhoods at a glance

Milan's historic centre is compact and largely walkable. The Duomo is the geographical and psychological centre of the city — from here, the main neighbourhoods radiate outward. Understanding their character helps with navigation and with choosing where to stay and eat.

Centro Storico (Duomo area): the most tourist-heavy zone, with the cathedral, Galleria and La Scala all within a few hundred metres. Most expensive for hotels and restaurants; most convenient for first-time visitors.

Brera: immediately north of the centre. Elegant, cobblestoned neighbourhood with the Pinacoteca di Brera, boutiques and excellent restaurants. The most atmospheric area for an evening out.

Castello / Sempione: northwest of the centre. Dominated by the Sforza castle and Parco Sempione. Also the neighbourhood of the Last Supper (Santa Maria delle Grazie) and the Triennale design museum.

Navigli: south of the centre. Canal district — Milan's most lively area for aperitivo, evening dining and nightlife. Accessible by M2 (Porta Genova).

Porta Venezia / Buenos Aires: east of the centre. Multicultural neighbourhood with Corso Buenos Aires shopping, the Giardini Pubblici and the GAM museum. Good value for hotels; connected to Linate Airport by M4.

Porta Nuova / Isola: north of the centre. The modern Milan — Piazza Gae Aulenti skyscraper district and the trendy Isola neighbourhood. Accessible by M2 or M5 (Garibaldi).

Porta Romana / Porta Ticinese: south and southeast. Less touristy, more local — good for authentic neighbourhood restaurants. Close to the Navigli area.

Distances from key points

The Milan city centre is compact by major European city standards. From the Duomo: Castello Sforzesco is a 20-minute walk west; the Pinacoteca di Brera is 15 minutes north; the Navigli is 25 minutes south on foot or 10 minutes by M2. The Last Supper at Santa Maria delle Grazie is 25 minutes west by foot or a short metro+walk combination.

Getting around with a map

The Metro map is the most useful navigational tool — see our Milan Metro guide for the full five-line network. For walking, the historic centre fits within a roughly 2km radius from the Duomo — surprisingly manageable on foot. The main thing that catches visitors off guard is that Milan's street grid is not rectangular but radial — streets fan out from the centre in all directions, which means that east-west and north-south movement sometimes requires diagonal routes.

Frequently asked questions

What is the centre of Milan?+

The geographical and symbolic centre of Milan is Piazza del Duomo — the square in front of the cathedral. From here, the Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II leads north to Piazza della Scala. The historic centre is compact — most major attractions are within a 20-minute walk of the Duomo.

Is Milan easy to navigate on foot?+

Yes — Milan's historic centre is very walkable. The Duomo, Galleria, La Scala, Brera gallery and Castello Sforzesco are all within comfortable walking distance of each other. The metro is most useful for reaching the airports, the Navigli and outlying areas. Walking is generally faster than any transport mode within the centre itself.

Which side of Milan is the Navigli?+

The Navigli canal district is in the south of Milan, roughly 2.5km south of the Duomo. It is accessible on foot in about 25 minutes from the Duomo, or by Metro M2 to Porta Genova (5 minutes from the Duomo station).

How far is Brera from the Duomo?+

Brera is approximately 800 metres north of the Duomo — about a 10–12 minute walk. The Pinacoteca di Brera is on Via Brera, easily reached on foot from the city centre.
