Piazza Mercanti

✓ Free · Open 24h

Piazza Mercanti (Merchants' Square) is one of the most charming and atmospheric squares in Milan — and one of the most overlooked. Located just steps from the Duomo, it preserves an authentic medieval atmosphere thanks to a remarkable collection of buildings dating back to the 13th century, when this square was the commercial and governmental heart of the city.

✍️ By Ivanka Garcia Mancebo · Updated June 2026 · ⏱️ 3 min read

The medieval heart of Milan

During the Middle Ages, Piazza Mercanti was the commercial and governmental centre of Milan. This is where justice was dispensed, trade was regulated and city life converged. While it no longer plays this role, the square remains a remarkably intact piece of medieval Milan — a quiet refuge just a few steps from the crowds at the Duomo.

The square is enclosed on all sides by medieval buildings, giving it an intimate, almost theatrical quality that is rare in any Italian city. It is one of the best-preserved medieval squares in northern Italy.

The five medieval buildings

Each of the buildings surrounding the square has a distinct history and character:

1233
Palazzo della Ragione
A magnificent red-brick building inaugurated in 1233 to house the courts of justice. The city's market was held beneath its impressive porticoes — the best-preserved Romanesque civic building in Milan.
1316
Loggia degli Osii
A striking building with large open arches decorated with statues and coats of arms. This was where judicial and notarial activities took place — its Gothic facade is one of the most beautiful in Milan.
1645
Palazzo delle Scuole Palatine
During the Middle Ages, this became the most important higher school in Milan. Many of the city's most famous figures were educated here.
14th c.
Casa dei Panigarola
A beautiful Gothic-styled building that served as a notary seat, particularly impressive for its porticoes. Today it houses a restaurant.
16th c.
Palazzo dei Giureconsulti
Located on the exterior of the square, this palace housed the Chamber of Commerce. The tower's clock was used to mark Milan's business hours for many years.

Milan's best-kept secret

Despite being just 243 metres from the Duomo, Piazza Mercanti sees a fraction of its visitors. Most tourists walk past without realising what lies behind the archway. This makes it one of the most rewarding detours in the entire city — a place where you can experience medieval Milan without crowds, noise or queues.

The square is particularly atmospheric in the early morning and on weekday afternoons, when locals use it as a shortcut and café-goers sit beneath the porticoes of the Palazzo della Ragione.

Visiting tips

  • It is impossible to miss if you walk between the Duomo and the Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II — look for the archway on Via Mercanti.
  • The square is at its most photogenic in the morning light, when the red brick of the Palazzo della Ragione glows warmly.
  • Allow 15–20 minutes to walk around and appreciate the buildings in detail — it is compact but densely packed with history.
  • Combine with the nearby Pinacoteca Ambrosiana (182 metres) for a perfect morning of medieval and Renaissance Milan.

Frequently asked questions

What is Piazza Mercanti in Milan?+

Piazza Mercanti (Merchants' Square) is Milan's most authentic medieval square, located just steps from the Duomo. During the Middle Ages it was the commercial and governmental heart of the city. Today it preserves five remarkable medieval buildings including the Palazzo della Ragione (1233).

Is Piazza Mercanti free to visit?+

Yes, Piazza Mercanti is a public square, completely free to visit and open 24 hours a day, all year round.

How far is Piazza Mercanti from the Duomo?+

Piazza Mercanti is approximately 243 metres from the Milan Cathedral — a 3-minute walk. It is one of the most convenient stops to combine with a Duomo visit.

What buildings are in Piazza Mercanti?+

Piazza Mercanti contains five remarkable medieval buildings: the Palazzo della Ragione (1233), the Loggia degli Osii (1316), the Palazzo delle Scuole Palatine, the Casa dei Panigarola and the Palazzo dei Giureconsulti.
