Museo Poldi Pezzoli

The Museo Poldi Pezzoli is one of Milan's most intimate and rewarding museums — a beautifully preserved 19th-century Milanese mansion that houses the extraordinary private art collection of Gian Giacomo Poldi Pezzoli. Small in size but exceptional in quality, it is one of the finest house museums in Italy.

✍️ By Ivanka Garcia Mancebo·Updated June 2026·⏱️ 3 min read

History

Gian Giacomo Poldi Pezzoli (1822–1879) was a young Milanese aristocrat whose passion for art and collecting led him to build one of the most remarkable private collections in 19th-century Italy. Drawing on a substantial inherited fortune, he spent decades assembling works of art, weapons, armour, tapestries, porcelain and furniture.

When he died in 1879, Poldi Pezzoli left his palazzo and his entire collection to the Brera Academy, with the express wish that it be opened to the public. The Museo Poldi Pezzoli was inaugurated in 1881. During World War II the museum was severely damaged and many paintings were destroyed; those that survived were restored, and the palazzo was rebuilt. It reopened to the public in 1951.

What to see

The collection is varied but carefully curated — paintings, sculptures, decorative arts, tapestries, porcelain, watches and an exceptional armory are all displayed in rooms that recreate the feel of the original aristocratic residence.

🖼️ The Golden Room
The museum's most important room, housing the finest paintings including Botticelli's Lamentation over the Dead Christ and Pollaiolo's Portrait of a Young Lady — one of the most reproduced images of the Italian Renaissance.
⚔️ The Armory
A magnificent hall displaying a large collection of weapons and armour from the medieval and Renaissance periods — one of the finest private armories in northern Italy and a highlight of any visit.
🏺 Decorative Arts
Porcelain, sculptures, tapestries and antique furniture displayed throughout the rooms, giving the museum its distinctive atmosphere of a lived-in aristocratic home.
🕰️ Watch Collection
An extraordinary collection of historical timepieces — a reminder that Poldi Pezzoli's passion extended far beyond paintings into all areas of decorative and mechanical art.

A small museum, a rich experience

The Poldi Pezzoli collection is compact but exceptional — the visit is comfortably done in 1–1.5 hours, making it an ideal addition to an afternoon that also includes Teatro alla Scala (257 metres away) or the Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II (367 metres). Unlike the larger galleries, the intimate scale of the museum means you can spend real time with individual works rather than rushing through vast halls.

Visiting tips

  • Closed on Tuesdays — plan your visit for any other day of the week.
  • Allow 1–1.5 hours — the collection is compact but warrants careful attention in each room.
  • Don't miss the armory — it is one of the most unexpected highlights and easy to rush past.
  • The Portrait of a Young Lady by Pollaiolo in the Golden Room is one of the most famous images of the Italian Renaissance — look for it carefully.
  • Combine with Teatro alla Scala (257 metres) and the Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II (367 metres) for a perfect cultural morning in central Milan.

Frequently asked questions

How much are Museo Poldi Pezzoli tickets?+

Adults €15, young people €10, over 65s €13. The museum is open Wednesday to Monday from 10am to 6pm. Closed on Tuesdays.

What are the opening hours?+

Wednesday to Monday from 10am to 6pm. Closed on Tuesdays.

What can you see in the Museo Poldi Pezzoli?+

The highlight is the Golden Room with Botticelli's Lamentation over the Dead Christ and Pollaiolo's Portrait of a Young Lady. The armory is exceptional. The museum also includes decorative arts, tapestries, porcelain and a historic watch collection.

How do I get to the Museo Poldi Pezzoli?+

By metro: Montenapoleone (line M3). By tram: Manzoni Croce Rossa (lines 1 and 2). By bus: lines 61 and 94. The museum is at Via Manzoni, 12, 257 metres from Teatro alla Scala.
