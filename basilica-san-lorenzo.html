Basilica of San Lorenzo

✓ Free entry

The Basilica of San Lorenzo Maggiore is Milan's oldest church, established during the 4th century and built using stones salvaged from earlier Roman structures. In front of it stand 16 ancient Roman columns from the 3rd century — the most visible Roman remains in Milan. Entry is free.

✍️ By Ivanka Garcia Mancebo · Updated June 2026 · ⏱️ 3 min read

The Colonne di San Lorenzo

Before entering the church, stop to appreciate what stands in front of it: the Colonne di San Lorenzo (Columns of San Lorenzo) — 16 monolithic Roman columns dating from the 3rd century AD, taken from an earlier Roman building (most likely a large public bath or temple complex) when the basilica was constructed.

These columns are among the most significant Roman remains in Milan and one of the most atmospheric spots in the city, particularly in the early evening when they are lit and surrounded by the vibrant Ticinese neighbourhood. A bronze statue of Constantine the Great — the first Roman Emperor to convert to Christianity — stands in the courtyard behind.

Inside the basilica

The interior of San Lorenzo is decorated with marble and impressive early mosaics. The church has a distinctive centrally planned layout — an unusual octagonal form that reflects the late Roman and early Byzantine architectural tradition in which it was built.

The highlight of the interior is the Chapel of Saint Aquilino (Cappella di Sant'Aquilino), a separate chapel adjoining the main nave and accessible through a doorway on the right. This 4th-century oratory is decorated with remarkable early Byzantine mosaics depicting Christ and the Apostles — among the oldest Christian mosaics in northern Italy.

🏛️ Roman foundations

From the Cappella di Sant'Aquilino, a staircase descends to a small crypt beneath the chapel where you can see the original Roman materials used in the construction of the church — including sections of wall and architectural fragments extracted from a Roman amphitheatre that once stood in this part of the ancient city.

The Ticinese neighbourhood

San Lorenzo stands on Corso di Porta Ticinese, in the heart of one of Milan's most characterful and bohemian neighbourhoods. The area around the columns is a favourite gathering spot for Milanese, especially in the evening — students, locals and visitors sit on the steps around the columns for the informal aperitivo hour that spontaneously forms here on warm evenings. It is one of the most authentic and unscripted experiences Milan offers.

Visiting tips

  • The columns are always visible from the outside and make for excellent photographs at any hour — especially at sunset and in the evening when they are lit.
  • Don't miss the Chapel of Saint Aquilino (small entrance fee may apply) — the mosaics are extraordinary and few visitors take the time to find them.
  • The staircase down to the Roman foundations beneath Sant'Aquilino is easy to miss — look for the door on the right inside the chapel.
  • The Ticinese area has excellent bars and restaurants for aperitivo — combine the visit with an early evening drink around the columns.
  • Combine with the nearby Basilica di Sant'Ambrogio (666 metres) for a morning dedicated to early Christian Milan.

Frequently asked questions

Is the Basilica of San Lorenzo free to visit?+

Yes, entry is completely free. Open Monday to Friday from 8am to 12:30pm and 3pm to 6:30pm; Saturday and Sunday from 9am to 1pm and 3pm to 7pm. The columns outside are always visible.

What are the Colonne di San Lorenzo?+

The Colonne di San Lorenzo are 16 ancient Roman columns standing in the square in front of the basilica. They date from the 3rd century AD and were taken from an earlier Roman building when the church was constructed. They are among the most visible Roman remains in Milan.

Is this the oldest church in Milan?+

Yes — the Basilica of San Lorenzo Maggiore is considered Milan's oldest church, established during the 4th century. It was built using stones and materials taken from earlier Roman structures on the site.

What is the Chapel of Saint Aquilino?+

The Cappella di Sant'Aquilino is a 4th-century chapel adjoining the basilica, decorated with early Byzantine mosaics depicting Christ and the Apostles. Beneath it is a crypt containing original Roman materials from a Roman amphitheatre that once stood here.

How do I get to the Basilica of San Lorenzo?+

By tram: line 3 stops at Ticinese Molino delle Armi, right in front of the columns. By metro: Sant'Ambrogio (line M2) is about a 10-minute walk through the Ticinese neighbourhood.
