San Bernardino alle Ossa

✓ Free entry

San Bernardino alle Ossa is one of the most unusual and memorable churches in Milan — a 13th-century ossuary chapel whose interior walls are decorated with human skulls and bones. Macabre to some, deeply fascinating to others, it is one of those places that stays with you long after you leave. Entry is free, and it is just a short walk from the Duomo.

✍️ By Ivanka Garcia Mancebo · Updated June 2026 · ⏱️ 3 min read

History

The story of San Bernardino alle Ossa begins in 1145, when a hospital and cemetery were built close to the Basilica of Santo Stefano Maggiore. The cemetery quickly became overwhelmed — Milan was a densely populated medieval city with high mortality rates — and it became necessary to build a dedicated hall to store the bones of the deceased.

In 1269 the Basilica of San Bernardino alle Ossa was built adjacent to this bone repository. Over the following centuries, the ossuary was decorated with the bones that had been accumulating there — arranged into elaborate patterns covering the walls of the chapel in what became a powerful memento mori: a reminder of human mortality.

Inside: the ossuary chapel

The ossuary chapel is a small, intimate space whose walls are entirely covered in human skulls and bones, arranged in intricate decorative patterns. Femurs, tibias and skulls are stacked and interleaved to create geometric designs — a tradition found in a handful of churches across Catholic Europe, most famously the Capuchin Crypt in Rome.

Rather than being gruesome, the effect is strangely beautiful and deeply contemplative. The bones were those of ordinary Milanese citizens — not saints or nobles — and the chapel served as a place for prayer and reflection on the transience of life. The ceiling fresco above the bone-covered walls, depicting souls ascending to heaven, completes the effect with unexpected grace.

💀 Memento mori

The tradition of decorating chapels with human bones — known as memento mori ("remember you will die") — was widespread in Catholic Europe from the medieval period. The intent was not to disturb visitors but to encourage reflection on mortality and the afterlife. San Bernardino alle Ossa is one of the finest and most accessible examples in Italy outside Rome.

Visiting tips

  • The ossuary chapel is a small space — a visit takes about 15–20 minutes, making it an easy detour from the Duomo.
  • Note the Sunday closing at midday — plan your visit for a weekday morning or Saturday afternoon.
  • The church is an active place of worship, so respectful behaviour is expected.
  • This is not recommended for very young children or anyone particularly sensitive to imagery of death.
  • Combine with the nearby Duomo (476 metres) and Piazza Mercanti (711 metres) for an interesting contrast of medieval and Gothic Milan.

Frequently asked questions

Is San Bernardino alle Ossa free to visit?+

Yes, entry is completely free. Open Monday to Friday from 8am to 6pm, Saturday from 9:30am to 6pm, and Sunday from 9:30am to 12pm only.

What is San Bernardino alle Ossa?+

It is a 13th-century ossuary chapel in central Milan, famous for its extraordinary interior decoration made entirely of human skulls and bones. It was built in 1269 to house the bones from an overflowing cemetery beside the nearby Basilica of Santo Stefano Maggiore.

Is it similar to the Capuchin Crypt in Rome?+

Yes — San Bernardino alle Ossa is often compared to the Santa Maria della Concezione dei Cappuccini in Rome. Both are ossuary chapels decorated with human bones as a memento mori. Milan's version is smaller but equally striking and far less visited.

How do I get to San Bernardino alle Ossa?+

By metro: Duomo station (lines M1 and M3), a 6-minute walk. By tram: lines 12, 23 and 27. By bus: lines 54 and 60. The address is Via Verziere, 2.
