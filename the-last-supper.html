The Last Supper by Leonardo da Vinci

Leonardo da Vinci's The Last Supper (Cenacolo Vinciano), created between 1495 and 1497, is one of the most famous paintings in the world. Visitors can see the authentic mural in the refectory of the Convent of Santa Maria delle Grazie — but entry must be booked well in advance.

✍️ By Ivanka Garcia Mancebo · Updated June 2026 · ⏱️ 5 min read

About the painting

The Last Supper is an enormous mural measuring 640 cm × 880 cm. Rather than using the standard fresco technique of the time, Leonardo da Vinci painted it in tempera and oil on a dry plaster wall — a choice that allowed him to rework the image but made it far more vulnerable to deterioration over time.

📷 Photography is strictly prohibited inside the viewing room

The creation of The Last Supper

At the end of the 15th century, Ludovico Sforza, Duke of Milan and Leonardo's patron, commissioned the work as part of his plan to convert the convent into a mausoleum for his family. Sforza was never able to realise his wish — he was handed over to the French by the Swiss and died imprisoned.

Leonardo investigated the theme relentlessly and made numerous sketches before beginning to paint. Those who witnessed him at work described erratic behaviour: sometimes painting from dawn without stopping for lunch, other times wandering the streets looking for people to use as models, or simply staring at the painting for hours in a daze.

Remarkably, da Vinci was never paid for this work, even after dedicating three years of his life to it — nor did he appear to want payment.

What the painting depicts

Leonardo chose to capture the precise moment when Jesus announces that one of his apostles will betray him — capturing the astonishment, horror and disbelief of all 12 disciples in a single, extraordinarily powerful composition.

Although da Vinci identified all the apostles in his sketches, some figures in the finished mural are ambiguous. The figure sitting directly to Jesus's right appears notably feminine in appearance, leading many art historians to believe it may represent Mary Magdalene rather than the apostle John. This interpretation was popularised by Dan Brown's novel The Da Vinci Code (2003) and its 2006 film adaptation starring Tom Hanks and Audrey Tautou.

🎨 Art curiosity

Da Vinci's choice to paint on dry plaster rather than wet fresco was highly unusual for the time. While it gave him the ability to work slowly and make corrections, the technique began to deteriorate almost immediately after completion — within decades of being finished, the painting was already flaking.

A survivor: deterioration and restoration

The painting has suffered considerably over the centuries. During the Napoleonic Wars, the refectory was used as a stable and the troops used the wall for target practice. In 1943, an Allied bombing raid destroyed the refectory roof, leaving the painting exposed to the elements for nearly three years.

After decades of painstaking restoration work, The Last Supper has recovered much of its original beauty. A major restoration project completed in 1999 — which took 21 years — brought it back to something approaching its original state.

Santa Maria delle Grazie

The church and convent of Santa Maria delle Grazie was established in 1463, commissioned by Duke Francesco I Sforza for the Dominican Order. It was originally designed by Guiniforte Solari and later completed by Bramante in 1492, who added a beautiful dome, a cloister, refectory and semi-circular apses.

Compared to other Milanese churches, Santa Maria delle Grazie is architecturally modest. It is entirely due to The Last Supper that the building has achieved worldwide renown. Both the church and the mural are listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

The sacristy also contains some pages of the Codex Atlanticus — a collection of writings and drawings by Leonardo da Vinci — on loan from the Pinacoteca Ambrosiana.

How to book your visit

Visits are strictly controlled: only 25 people are admitted every 15 minutes, and each visit lasts exactly 15 minutes. Total daily capacity is very limited — tickets routinely sell out weeks, sometimes months, in advance.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need to book The Last Supper tickets in advance?+

Yes, absolutely. Entry is by timed ticket only, with groups of just 25 people admitted every 15 minutes. Tickets sell out weeks — sometimes months — in advance. You must book before you travel. Do not arrive without a ticket.

How much do Last Supper tickets cost?+

Standard tickets cost €26 for adults and €13 for young people aged 18–25. EU citizens under 18 enter free. A guided tour with skip-the-line access costs from €89.

What are the opening hours for The Last Supper?+

Tuesday to Saturday from 9am to 7pm. Sunday from 9am to 1:45pm. Closed on Mondays, 1st January and 25th December.

Can you take photos of The Last Supper?+

No. Photography is strictly prohibited inside the viewing room. You must leave bags and cameras at the cloakroom before entering. The rule is strictly enforced.

How long does a visit to The Last Supper last?+

Each visit lasts exactly 15 minutes. Groups rotate in and out on a strict timetable. A guided tour that combines The Last Supper with a walking tour of central Milan runs for approximately 3 hours in total.
