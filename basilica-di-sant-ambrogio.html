Basilica di Sant'Ambrogio

✓ Free entry

The Basilica di Sant'Ambrogio is Milan's oldest and most important church — and one of the most significant in all of Italy. Consecrated in 387 AD by Saint Ambrose himself, it was later reconstructed in the Lombard Romanesque style in the 11th century, becoming the architectural model for churches across northern Italy for generations.

✍️ By Ivanka Garcia Mancebo · Updated June 2026 · ⏱️ 4 min read

History and significance

The basilica was consecrated in 387 AD by Saint Ambrose, Bishop of Milan — one of the most influential figures in the early Christian church and one of the four original Doctors of the Catholic Faith. Ambrose himself is buried in the crypt beneath the church he founded, alongside two other saints.

During the 11th century the church was comprehensively reconstructed in what would become known as the Lombard Romanesque style — a blend of Roman civic architecture with early Christian and Byzantine influences. The basilica was the first church in the world to adopt this style, which then spread across northern Italy and beyond.

What to see inside

The exterior is striking and unusual — two asymmetrical red brick bell towers flank an enormous atrium that is nearly as large as the church itself and contains several archaeological remains from earlier structures on the site.

Inside, the Stilicho's Sepulchre is one of the finest early Christian artworks in Milan — a magnificent 4th-century sarcophagus decorated with elaborate high-relief carvings, traditionally associated with the Roman general Stilicho and his family.

At the end of the southern nave is the Oratory of San Vittorio in Ciel d'Oro — a small chapel built before the first basilica, on the site of an early Christian cemetery. Its golden mosaic ceiling, dating to the 5th century, is one of the oldest surviving works of its kind in Milan.

⛪ The crypt

The basilica's crypt contains the remains of three saints: Saint Ambrose, Saint Gervasius and Saint Protasius. All three are dressed in full episcopal and martyrs' attire and displayed in a glass reliquary — one of the most remarkable sights in any church in Italy.

Sant'Ambrogio: patron saint of Milan

Sant'Ambrogio (Saint Ambrose) is the patron saint of Milan. He was Bishop of Milan from 374 to 397 AD and is celebrated for his theology, his music (he composed the first Christian hymns), and his political courage in standing up to the Roman Emperor. His feast day, 7 December, is a public holiday in Milan — and traditionally marks the opening night of the La Scala opera season, one of the most important events in the Milanese calendar.

Visiting tips

  • The basilica closes at midday and reopens at 2:30pm — plan your visit accordingly. Sunday opening is only 3–5pm.
  • The atrium alone is worth spending time in — look for the early medieval carved capitals and inscriptions on the columns.
  • The Oratory of San Vittorio in Ciel d'Oro (golden ceiling mosaic) is easy to miss — it is at the far end of the southern nave.
  • Combine with the nearby Last Supper (587 metres) for a morning dedicated to Milan's most significant religious and artistic heritage.
  • Dress code is enforced — shoulders and knees must be covered. Scarves are available at the entrance.

Frequently asked questions

Is the Basilica di Sant'Ambrogio free to visit?+

Yes, entry is completely free. The basilica is open Monday to Saturday from 10am to 12pm and 2:30pm to 6pm, and on Sundays from 3pm to 5pm only.

When was the Basilica di Sant'Ambrogio built?+

The basilica was consecrated in 387 AD by Saint Ambrose, Bishop of Milan. It was later reconstructed in the Lombard Romanesque style in the 11th century, becoming the model for this architectural style throughout northern Italy.

Who is Sant'Ambrogio?+

Sant'Ambrogio (Saint Ambrose) is the patron saint of Milan. He was Bishop of Milan from 374 to 397 AD and is one of the four original Doctors of the Catholic Church. His feast day, 7 December, is a public holiday in Milan and marks the opening night of the La Scala opera season.

What is inside the Basilica di Sant'Ambrogio?+

The basilica contains Stilicho's Sepulchre (a 4th-century sarcophagus), the crypt with the remains of three saints (Saint Ambrose, Saint Gervasius and Saint Protasius), and the Oratory of San Vittorio in Ciel d'Oro with its 5th-century golden mosaic ceiling. The enormous atrium also contains archaeological remains.

Is there a dress code for the Basilica di Sant'Ambrogio?+

Yes — shoulders and knees must be covered to enter the basilica. Scarves are available at the entrance if needed. The rule is strictly enforced.
