Museo Nazionale della Scienza e della Tecnologia "Leonardo da Vinci"

The Museo Nazionale della Scienza e della Tecnologia "Leonardo da Vinci" is Italy's largest science and technology museum — a fascinating institution housed in a 6th-century monastery near Sant'Ambrogio. Its collections span physics, optics, astronomy, transport and communications, with a dedicated gallery of wooden models of Leonardo da Vinci's inventions.

✍️ By Ivanka Garcia Mancebo·Updated June 2026·⏱️ 3 min read

History

The museum is housed in the former Monastery of San Vittore al Corpo, built in the 6th century and extended during the Renaissance. The monastery was previously used as a hospital and military barracks before being transformed into a museum in 1953.

Founded to document the evolution of science and technology throughout history, the museum takes its name from Leonardo da Vinci — a nod to his connections with Milan, where he lived for nearly two decades and produced some of his most important scientific and artistic work.

What to see

The museum is spread across several buildings and has an unusually broad scope. The highlights:

🎨 Leonardo da Vinci Gallery
The most visited section — wooden scale models of Leonardo's inventions built from his original drawings. Flying machines, military devices, hydraulic tools and more.
🚂 Transport section
Contains Italy's first locomotive, a large historic sailing vessel and a real submarine — one of the most impressive transport collections in Europe.
🔭 Science galleries
Physics, optics, acoustics, astronomy, timekeeping, photography and cinema — a sweeping survey of Italian and European scientific achievement.
👧 Interactive experiences
Extensive interactive galleries allow visitors — especially children — to engage directly with scientific principles. One of the best family museums in Milan.

Who is it for?

The museum is excellent for families with children, for science and technology enthusiasts, and for anyone with an interest in Leonardo da Vinci beyond his paintings. The interactive galleries and the spectacular transport section — especially the submarine and the locomotive — make it a genuinely engaging experience for visitors of all ages. For general tourists with limited time, it may be less essential than Milan's art museums.

Visiting tips

  • Closed on Mondays, 1 January and 24–25 December.
  • Allow at least 2–3 hours — the museum is very large and spread across multiple buildings.
  • The transport section (submarine and locomotive) is in a separate building from the main monastery — follow the signage carefully.
  • The Leonardo da Vinci gallery is the most visited section and can get crowded on weekends — visit in the morning if possible.
  • Combine with the nearby Basilica di Sant'Ambrogio (414 metres) and The Last Supper (365 metres) for a full day in this area of Milan.

Frequently asked questions

What is the Museo della Scienza e della Tecnologia in Milan?+

It is Italy's largest science and technology museum, founded in 1953 in a 6th-century monastery near Sant'Ambrogio. It contains collections on physics, optics, astronomy, transport and communications, plus a dedicated Leonardo da Vinci gallery.

How much are tickets?+

Adults €10, children aged 3–26 and seniors over 65 €7.50, children under 3 free.

What are the opening hours?+

Tuesday to Friday: 9:30am to 5pm. Saturday, Sunday and holidays: 9:30am to 6:30pm. Closed Mondays, 1 January and 24–25 December.

What is the Leonardo da Vinci gallery?+

A dedicated section displaying wooden scale models of Leonardo's inventions, built from his original drawings — flying machines, military devices, hydraulic tools and more. One of the most popular sections of the museum.

Is the science museum good for children?+

Yes — it is one of the best family museums in Milan. The interactive science galleries, the real submarine and locomotive in the transport section, and the Leonardo models make it engaging for children of all ages.
