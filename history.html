History of Milan

From Celtic settlement to Roman imperial capital — 2,600 years of a city that shaped Western civilisation

✍️ By Ivanka Garcia Mancebo·Updated June 2026

Few cities in the world have played as central a role in European history as Milan. Over 2,600 years it has been a Celtic tribal capital, a Roman imperial seat, a Renaissance court, a Napoleonic showcase city, a crucible of Italian national identity, a target of Allied bombing and, eventually, one of the world's great centres of fashion, design and finance. Understanding this history makes the city infinitely richer to explore.

Celtic and Roman origins (600 BC – 400 AD)

Milan's story begins around 600 BC, when Celtic tribes of the Insubres settled on the fertile plain of the Po Valley and named their new home Medhelan — believed to mean "middle of the plain" or possibly "rich land." The Romans conquered the settlement in 222 BC, refounding it as Mediolanum and rapidly developing it into the most important city in their Cisalpine Gaul province.

By the late Roman period, Mediolanum had grown into something far more significant. In 286 AD, Emperor Diocletian divided the vast Roman Empire for administrative purposes and made Milan the capital of the Western Roman Empire — a position it would hold until 402 AD, when Ravenna took over due to its more defensible location. During this period Milan rivalled Rome in grandeur, with an enormous amphitheatre (partially visible today near the Archaeological Museum), a circus larger than the Circus Maximus in Rome, imperial palaces, baths and city walls.

The most historically consequential moment of this era came in 313 AD, when Emperor Constantine issued the Edict of Milan from the city — the decree that legalised Christianity throughout the Roman Empire and fundamentally changed the course of Western civilisation. Visitors who want to understand the weight of this moment should visit the Milan Archaeological Museum, where the visible remains of the Roman city wall bring Mediolanum's imperial grandeur into sharp focus.

Shortly after, in 374 AD, a remarkable man named Ambrose was elected Bishop of Milan by popular acclamation — despite not yet being baptised. Ambrose transformed the city into one of the great centres of Christian theology, wrote some of the most influential texts in the early Church, and is credited with introducing congregational hymn-singing to the Western Church. He was later declared the patron saint of Milan; his feast day, 7 December (Sant'Ambrogio), remains a public holiday in the city today, and the Basilica di Sant'Ambrogio — built over his tomb — is one of the finest Romanesque churches in Italy.

The Visconti, the Sforza and the Renaissance (1277–1535)

After the fall of the Western Roman Empire and centuries of instability — Lombard invasion, Carolingian rule, communal self-government — Milan came under the dynastic control of the Visconti family in 1277. The Visconti transformed the city into one of the most powerful city-states in Italy, commissioning works of extraordinary ambition: the Milan Cathedral (begun in 1386, funded by the Visconti as a vow to the Virgin) is the defining monument of their rule — the largest Gothic cathedral in the world by volume, taking over 500 years to complete.

In 1450, the condottiere Francesco Sforza seized power after the death of the last Visconti duke, founding the Sforza dynasty — and initiating one of the most remarkable chapters in European cultural history. Under Ludovico Sforza (il Moro), Milan became a leading centre of the Italian Renaissance. The court attracted some of the greatest minds of the age — most famously Leonardo da Vinci, who arrived in Milan in 1482 and remained for nearly two decades. During this period Leonardo painted the Last Supper in the refectory of Santa Maria delle Grazie, produced the vast technical notebooks that became the Codex Atlanticus (now in the Biblioteca Ambrosiana), and served the Sforza court as engineer, architect, musician and pageant designer. The cultural richness of this period is visible throughout the city today.

Spanish rule, Napoleon and the Risorgimento (1535–1861)

The fall of Ludovico Sforza to French invasion in 1499 began a long period of instability and foreign domination. From 1535, Milan became part of the Spanish Empire — a period of relative stagnation that lasted nearly 180 years. The city was administered competently if not brilliantly, and the Counter-Reformation brought significant religious building activity, including the frescoed church of San Maurizio al Monastero Maggiore, one of the great artistic achievements of the period.

The arrival of Napoleon Bonaparte in 1796 transformed the city almost overnight. Napoleon created the Cisalpine Republic (later the Kingdom of Italy with Milan as its capital), commissioned the construction of wide new boulevards, public buildings and the Arena Civica, and personally crowned himself King of Italy in the Duomo in 1805. It was Napoleon who enriched the Pinacoteca di Brera with works confiscated from churches and monasteries — a controversial act that gave the gallery much of its current magnificence.

After Napoleon's defeat and the restoration of Austrian rule, Milan became a hotbed of liberal and nationalist sentiment. The poet Alessandro Manzoni wrote I Promessi Sposi here; the composer Giuseppe Verdi used the city's opera house (La Scala, inaugurated 1778) to make veiled political statements. The Cinque Giornate di Milano — the Five Days of Milan in March 1848 — saw a popular uprising that temporarily drove the Austrians from the city. Though suppressed, it became a founding myth of the Italian Risorgimento. In 1859, following the Second Italian War of Independence, Milan was finally incorporated into the Kingdom of Sardinia, and in 1861 into the newly unified Kingdom of Italy.

Industrialisation, World War II and the Miracolo Economico (1861–present)

Unified Italy found Milan already its most economically dynamic city. The late 19th and early 20th centuries brought rapid industrialisation, immigration from southern Italy, and the construction of the magnificent Milano Centrale railway station (completed 1931 under Mussolini — its monumental Art Deco Fascist architecture remains one of the most striking interiors in Italy).

During World War II, Milan suffered devastating Allied bombing campaigns in 1943 that destroyed much of the historic centre and many of its industrial facilities. The city was also a centre of the Italian Resistance movement; in April 1945, Benito Mussolini was captured near Lake Como while attempting to flee to Switzerland, and executed by partisans. His body was displayed in Piazzale Loreto — a square in the northeast of the city.

The postwar recovery was extraordinary. From the 1950s, Milan led Italy's miracolo economico — the economic miracle — becoming the engine of one of the fastest industrial transformations in European history. Companies such as Fiat, Pirelli, Olivetti and Alfa Romeo made their headquarters here; the publishing industry, advertising and design sectors flourished. By the 1970s and 1980s, Milan had established itself as the global capital of fashion (Giorgio Armani, Gianni Versace, Dolce & Gabbana) and design (the annual Salone del Mobile, now the world's most important design fair).

Today Milan is Italy's financial centre, home to the Borsa Italiana stock exchange, the headquarters of most major Italian corporations and international banks, and one of Europe's most cosmopolitan and innovative cities. Its population of 1.4 million (3.2 million in the metropolitan area) is among the most diverse in Italy, and the city continues to reinvent itself — most visibly in the Porta Nuova development around Piazza Gae Aulenti, which transformed a former railway yard into a forest of futuristic skyscrapers that have become new symbols of contemporary Milan.

Frequently asked questions

What is Milan historically famous for?+

Milan has been historically significant as the capital of the Western Roman Empire (286–402 AD), the city from which Constantine issued the Edict of Milan legalising Christianity (313 AD), a major Renaissance court where Leonardo da Vinci worked for nearly 20 years, and the birthplace of Risorgimento sentiment that led to Italian unification in 1861.

What was Roman Milan called?+

Roman Milan was called Mediolanum — derived from the Celtic name for the settlement. It served as the capital of the Western Roman Empire from 286 to 402 AD and was the most important city in Roman Italy after Rome itself during that period.

Who built the Milan Cathedral?+

The Milan Cathedral (Duomo) was begun in 1386 under the commission of Gian Galeazzo Visconti, the ruler of Milan. Construction continued for over 500 years — it was only fully completed in the 19th century under Napoleon, who ordered the façade finished for his coronation as King of Italy in 1805.
