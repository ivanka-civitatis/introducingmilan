Public Holidays in Milan

Italian national holidays plus Sant'Ambrogio — Milan's own patron saint celebration

✍️ By Ivanka Garcia Mancebo·Updated June 2026

Italian national public holidays

1 January — New Year's Day
Capodanno. Most attractions closed or with reduced hours. Piazza del Duomo hosts New Year celebrations the night before.
6 January — Epiphany
La Befana — the Italian Christmas. More important than Christmas Day for many Italians. Shops and most services closed.
Easter Sunday & Monday
Pasqua and Pasquetta. Easter Monday is a public holiday — many Milanese take day trips to the lakes or countryside.
25 April — Liberation Day
Festa della Liberazione, commemorating the end of Nazi-Fascist occupation in 1945. Parades and events across the city.
1 May — Labour Day
Festa dei Lavoratori. Almost everything closed. The metro runs reduced hours (7am–7:30pm).
2 June — Republic Day
Festa della Repubblica. Military parade in Rome; celebrations across Italy.
15 August — Ferragosto
The most important summer holiday — virtually everything closes and Milan empties. Avoid visiting unless specifically for the empty city experience.
1 November — All Saints' Day
Ognissanti. Public holiday; Cimitero Monumentale is particularly visited on this day.
8 December — Immaculate Conception
Immacolata Concezione. Public holiday — Christmas season traditionally begins with this day.
25–26 December — Christmas
Natale and Santo Stefano. Both days are public holidays. Museums and attractions mostly closed on 25 December; reduced hours on 26.

Sant'Ambrogio Day — Milan's own holiday

In addition to the national holidays, Milan celebrates 7 December as the feast day of Sant'Ambrogio — the city's patron saint. This is a local public holiday observed only in Milan: schools and offices close, and the day marks the traditional opening of the La Scala opera season — one of the most important nights in the global opera calendar. The city has a particularly festive atmosphere around Piazza del Duomo and the Basilica di Sant'Ambrogio.

A large outdoor market called the O bei! O bei! is held around the castle and in the surrounding streets during the first weeks of December — one of the most enjoyable Christmas markets in northern Italy.

What is open on public holidays in Milan?

On Italian public holidays (giorni festivi), most shops, offices and many restaurants close. However, tourist attractions, museums and monuments generally remain open — often with special hours. The key exceptions are 1 January (virtually everything closed), 1 May (Labour Day — almost everything closed, metro runs reduced hours 7am–7:30pm) and 25 December (Christmas Day — museums mostly closed).

The situation on 26 December (Santo Stefano, a public holiday) is better than Christmas Day itself — many museums reopen with reduced hours. On 6 January (Epiphany), most tourist attractions are open. In practice, the public holidays that cause the most disruption for visitors are 1 May, 1 January and 25 December.

The Sant'Ambrogio celebrations in detail

The 7 December festivities around the feast of Sant'Ambrogio are one of the most characteristically Milanese events in the city's calendar. The day begins with the opening night of the La Scala opera season — one of the most important events in the global opera calendar, attended by politicians, celebrities and the Milanese social elite, and traditionally accompanied by protests outside the theatre. Later in the day, the Basilica di Sant'Ambrogio holds solemn services attended by the city's civic authorities.

Throughout the first two weeks of December, the O bei! O bei! market is held in the streets around Castello Sforzesco — a large outdoor market selling antiques, crafts, food and seasonal decorations. It is one of the most enjoyable Christmas markets in northern Italy and free to visit.

Bridge holidays — "ponti"

Italians are skilled at turning public holidays into long weekends by taking the intervening working day off — these extended breaks are called ponti (bridges). When a public holiday falls on a Thursday or Tuesday, many Italians take the adjacent Friday or Monday as annual leave, creating a 4-day break. The most common ponti are around 25 April (Liberation Day), 2 June (Republic Day) and 1 November (All Saints' Day). During these periods, Milan can be notably quiet as locals travel, but tourist attractions remain open.

School holidays and their effect on tourism

Italian school holidays do not follow a fixed national pattern — they vary by region and year. In Lombardy, the main school holidays are: Christmas (approximately 23 December–7 January), Easter (the week around Easter), and summer (mid-June to mid-September). The summer school holiday period coincides with the busiest tourist season. Spring and autumn half-terms are short (3–5 days) and have minimal impact on tourist crowds.

Frequently asked questions

What are the public holidays in Milan?+

Italian national public holidays: 1 January (New Year's Day), 6 January (Epiphany), Easter Sunday and Monday, 25 April (Liberation Day), 1 May (Labour Day), 2 June (Republic Day), 15 August (Ferragosto), 1 November (All Saints' Day), 8 December (Immaculate Conception), 25–26 December (Christmas and Santo Stefano). Plus 7 December (Sant'Ambrogio) — a local holiday observed only in Milan.

Is 7 December a public holiday in Milan?+

Yes — 7 December, the feast day of Sant'Ambrogio (Milan's patron saint), is a local public holiday observed only in Milan. Schools and most offices close. The day marks the traditional opening of the La Scala opera season and is accompanied by the O bei! O bei! Christmas market near Castello Sforzesco.

Are museums open on public holidays in Milan?+

Most museums and tourist attractions remain open on public holidays, often with special hours. The main exceptions are 1 January, 1 May and 25 December, when most attractions close or operate on very reduced hours. Always check individual museum websites before visiting on a public holiday.

When is Ferragosto in Italy?+

Ferragosto is 15 August — the most important summer holiday in Italy. Many Milanese leave the city for the mountains or coast, and a significant number of smaller restaurants and shops close around this date (sometimes for the entire first two weeks of August). Major tourist attractions and museums generally remain open.

What is the O bei! O bei! market in Milan?+

The O bei! O bei! is a traditional Christmas market held in the streets around Castello Sforzesco and Parco Sempione during the first two weeks of December. It coincides with the Sant'Ambrogio festivities (7 December) and is one of the most enjoyable seasonal markets in northern Italy. Free to visit.
