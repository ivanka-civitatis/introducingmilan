Pinacoteca di Brera

The Pinacoteca di Brera is considered one of the finest art galleries in Italy — a museum of extraordinary depth, housed in a magnificent 17th-century palazzo in the heart of Milan's most elegant neighbourhood. Its 38 halls contain masterpieces by Raphael, Caravaggio, Tintoretto, Mantegna and Piero della Francesca.

✍️ By Ivanka Garcia Mancebo·Updated June 2026·⏱️ 4 min read

History

The Palazzo Brera was built during the 16th and 17th centuries, originally as a church and monastery for the Humiliati religious order, called Santa Maria in Brera. When the Humiliati were suppressed, the building became a Jesuit college and cultural venue, housing a library and an astronomical observatory.

In 1773 the Jesuits were suppressed in turn, and Archduchess Maria Theresa of Austria opened an Accademia di Belle Arti in the palazzo. In 1882 the Pinacoteca became independent from the Academy, and its collection has grown every year since.

The masterpieces

The paintings are arranged chronologically through 38 rooms, grouped by artistic school. The collection is overwhelmingly Italian and predominantly religious in subject — which makes it exceptional for anyone with an interest in Italian Renaissance and Baroque painting, though less immediately accessible to general audiences.

Raphael
The Marriage of the Virgin
Painted in 1504 when Raphael was just 21. One of the most perfectly composed works of the High Renaissance — an entire architectural world in a single painting.
Andrea Mantegna
The Lamentation of Christ
Perhaps the most studied painting in Brera. The radical foreshortening of Christ's body has been analysed for centuries by mathematicians, artists and doctors.
Tintoretto
Finding of the Body of St Mark
A dramatic, spatially complex masterpiece of Venetian Mannerism — one of the great narrative paintings of the 16th century.
Francesco Hayez
The Kiss
Painted in 1859 and the most reproduced Italian Romantic painting in history. The couple's embrace is widely read as a symbol of the Italian Risorgimento.
🏛️ The courtyard

The courtyard of Palazzo Brera is free to visit without purchasing a museum ticket. At its centre stands a remarkable neo-classical bronze statue of Napoleon by Antonio Canova (a later cast) — a reminder that Napoleon was personally responsible for enriching the Brera collection with works confiscated from churches and monasteries across northern Italy.

The Brera neighbourhood

The Pinacoteca is the cultural heart of Brera — one of Milan's most charming and atmospheric neighbourhoods. Cobblestone streets, independent galleries, antique shops, aperitivo bars and some of the city's finest restaurants surround the palazzo. A visit to the gallery combines naturally with an afternoon exploring the neighbourhood.

Visiting tips

  • The collection is primarily religious art from the 13th to 19th centuries — go with the right expectations and it is extraordinary. If you prefer secular or modern art, the Poldi Pezzoli or the Triennale may suit you better.
  • The guided tour is strongly recommended — many of the works require context to be fully appreciated.
  • The courtyard (with the Napoleon statue) is free — worth a visit even if you skip the museum.
  • The Mantegna Lamentation and the Raphael Marriage of the Virgin are in separate rooms — look for them on the floor map at the entrance.
  • Tuesday and Wednesday afternoons offer a reduced €7.50 rate for over 70s.

Frequently asked questions

How much are Pinacoteca di Brera tickets?+

Adults €15, EU citizens aged 18–25 €10, over 70s on Tuesdays and Wednesdays €7.50, under 18s and people with disabilities free. A guided tour with ticket costs €64.90 with Civitatis.

What are the opening hours?+

Tuesday to Sunday from 8:30am to 7:15pm. Closed on Mondays and on 25 December.

What are the most famous paintings in the Pinacoteca di Brera?+

The five most celebrated works are: The Marriage of the Virgin (Raphael), The Lamentation of Christ (Mantegna), Finding of the Body of St Mark (Tintoretto), Holy Conversation (Piero della Francesca) and The Kiss (Francesco Hayez).

Is the courtyard free to visit?+

Yes — the courtyard of Palazzo Brera is free to visit and does not require a museum ticket. It contains a bronze statue of Napoleon by Canova.
