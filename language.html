Language in Milan

Italian phrases for tourists — greetings, food, transport and emergencies

✍️ By Ivanka Garcia Mancebo·Updated June 2026

The language

The official language of Milan is Italian. As Italy's international business capital, Milan has a higher level of English proficiency than most Italian cities — in hotels, major restaurants and tourist areas, you will generally manage with English. However, outside the city centre and in smaller establishments, Italian remains essential.

Milanese people generally appreciate any effort to speak Italian, however limited. Even knowing a handful of basic phrases makes a significant difference to how you are received.

Essential phrases

👋 Greetings
Buongiorno — Good morning · Buonasera — Good evening · Ciao — Hello/Goodbye (informal) · Arrivederci — Goodbye (formal) · Per favore — Please · Grazie — Thank you · Prego — You're welcome
🍽️ Eating out
Un tavolo per due — A table for two · Il menù, per favore — The menu, please · Cosa consiglia? — What do you recommend? · Il conto, per favore — The bill, please · Senz'aglio — Without garlic

Dialects

Milanese (Milanes or Lombard) is a Romance dialect traditionally spoken in Milan, distinct from standard Italian. You may encounter it on historic shop signs and in some contexts, but standard Italian is universal throughout the city. No knowledge of Milanese dialect is needed for a visit.

Alphabet and pronunciation notes

Italian pronunciation is largely consistent — letters are generally pronounced as written. Key points: c before e/i is pronounced "ch" (like chiesa); g before e/i is "j" (like gelato); gli is like the "lli" in million; gn is like the "ny" in canyon. Double consonants are pronounced distinctly longer.

How much English is spoken in Milan?

Milan has a higher level of English proficiency than most Italian cities — a consequence of its role as an international business and fashion hub. In hotels, English is almost universally spoken to a good standard. In restaurants and tourist areas, you will manage with English in the vast majority of cases. In museums, many have English-language audioguides and some staff who speak English.

Outside the centre, in neighbourhood trattorias and smaller shops, Italian remains essential. The good news is that Milanese people are generally patient with foreigners attempting Italian, however imperfectly — a simple buongiorno and grazie will be warmly appreciated. The worst outcome is that someone speaks back to you in English, which means you have been helpful about it.

Essential Italian for eating out

Eating out in Italy follows a specific rhythm. You will be asked Quanti siete? (How many of you?) when you arrive. Say siamo in due (we are two), siamo in tre (three), and so on. To order, use vorrei... (I would like...) — more polite than voglio (I want). To ask for the bill: Il conto, per favore. To ask if service is included: Il servizio è incluso? Water comes in two varieties: acqua naturale (still) and acqua frizzante (sparkling).

A note on coffee: in Italy, a caffè is an espresso — small, strong and drunk standing at the bar. A caffè macchiato has a drop of milk. A cappuccino is only drunk at breakfast (never after a meal — Italians find this odd). Caffè americano is the closest to filter coffee. Asking for a "latte" will get you a glass of milk.

Italian pronunciation guide

Italian is almost entirely phonetic — words are pronounced as written. The main rules to remember: c before e or i = "ch" (as in chiesa = "kee-AY-za"); g before e or i = "j" (as in gelato = "jeh-LA-toh"); ch = hard "k" (as in Chianti); gh = hard "g"; gn = "ny" (as in gnocchi = "NYO-kee"); gli = "lyee" (as in famiglia = "fa-MEE-lyya"); double consonants are held longer than single ones — anno sounds different from ano. Stress generally falls on the second-to-last syllable.

Numbers and useful quantities

uno (1), due (2), tre (3), quattro (4), cinque (5), sei (6), sette (7), otto (8), nove (9), dieci (10), venti (20), trenta (30), cento (100), mille (1,000). For prices: quanto costa? (how much?) — costa tre euro (it costs three euros). Troppo caro (too expensive) is occasionally useful.

Frequently asked questions

Do people speak English in Milan?+

Yes, to a reasonable standard in tourist areas, hotels and larger restaurants. Milan has higher English proficiency than most Italian cities due to its international business environment. Outside the centre, Italian is more useful. Any effort to speak Italian — however basic — will be appreciated.

What language do people speak in Milan?+

The official language is Italian. A distinct local dialect, Milanese (part of the Lombard language family), was historically spoken in the city but is now rarely heard in everyday life. Standard Italian is universal throughout Milan.

Is it rude to speak English in Italy?+

No — Italians are generally welcoming to foreign visitors. That said, beginning any interaction with a greeting in Italian (Buongiorno, Buonasera) before switching to English is considered polite and will usually be met with warmth. Attempting even a few Italian words is always appreciated.

How do you say 'thank you' in Italian?+

Grazie (GRAT-see-eh). For 'you're welcome': Prego. For 'please': Per favore. For 'excuse me/sorry': Scusi (to a stranger) or Scusa (to a friend). For 'I don't understand': Non capisco. For 'Do you speak English?': Parla inglese?

What is the difference between 'buongiorno' and 'buonasera'?+

Buongiorno (good morning/good day) is used until approximately 1pm or when the working day transitions to afternoon — roughly 5–6pm. Buonasera (good evening) is used from late afternoon onwards. Using the wrong one is not offensive — Italians understand that foreigners mix them up — but using the correct one will be noticed positively.
