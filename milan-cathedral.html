Milan Cathedral

Milan Cathedral, called Duomo di Milano in Italian, is one of the world's largest Gothic cathedrals, located in the heart of the city. It is 515 ft (157 m) long and 302 ft (92 m) wide, and can house up to 40,000 people.

✍️ By Ivanka Garcia Mancebo · Updated June 2026 · ⏱️ 5 min read

The building and its construction

The site where the Cathedral stands was previously occupied by the Basilica of Sant'Ambrogio. In 838 the Basilica di Santa Tecla was founded next to the first church, but in 1075 a fire devastated both buildings. A few centuries later, the Duomo was established in the same location.

The construction of Milan Cathedral began in 1386, coinciding with Gian Galeazzo Visconti coming into power. The purpose was to modernize the area and celebrate the expansion of the Visconti territory. The Cathedral took five centuries to complete. During this time, various architects, sculptors, and artists contributed to the famous construction program known as the "Fabbrica del Duomo", merging the international Gothic style with the Lombard Romanesque.

The structure is covered with white and pink marble from the Candoglia quarry in Val D'Ossola. On the highest spire stands the Madonnina, a gilded bronze statue sculpted by Giuseppe Perego in 1774 — the symbol of Milan.

Inside the Cathedral

Milan Cathedral is a vast structure made of large darkened marble plates. The interior is elegant and stately thanks to its large sculpted marble columns that reach the ceiling. Large paintings adorn the walls, representing scenes from the Bible.

Among the highlights is the statue of Bartholomew the Apostle, depicted with his skin hanging off his shoulders as a reference to his martyrdom. One of the most precious objects is a nail said to have been used during the Crucifixion of Christ, housed in the dome behind the altar and exposed to the public only once a year, on the Saturday closest to 14 September.

Rooftop terrace

The rooftop terrace is practically as large as the whole roof of the Cathedral. Visitors can walk along the terrace while enjoying one of the most beautiful panoramic views of Milan, and see the roof's intricate sculptures up close. You can climb on foot or use the elevator for a supplement.

Crypt, Treasure and Baptistery

The crypt is located in the Chapel of Saint Charles Borromeo, where his remains are buried. Under the Duomo, visitors can also explore the archaeological remains of the Basilica di Santa Tecla and the ruins of a Christian baptistery from the fourth century, including a large octagonal baptismal font where, according to legend, Saint Ambrose baptized Saint Augustine in 387.

Milan Cathedral: the most visited attraction in Italy

Milan Cathedral is the most visited monument in Italy, welcoming over six million visitors a year — more than the Colosseum in Rome or the Uffizi Gallery in Florence. Its combination of extraordinary Gothic architecture, rich history, and central location makes it the undisputed symbol of the city.

Visiting tips

  • Even in summer, wear clothing that covers shoulders and knees — otherwise entry will be refused.
  • Buy tickets in advance online to avoid long queues at the entrance.
  • The rooftop at sunset offers spectacular views over the city and the Alps on a clear day.
  • Combined tickets (Cathedral + terrace + museum) offer the best value.

Frequently asked questions

What are the opening hours of Milan Cathedral? +

Milan Cathedral is open every day from 6:50 a.m. to 8:30 p.m. The rooftop terrace may have slightly different hours depending on the season.

How much does it cost to visit Milan Cathedral? +

A combined ticket for the Cathedral, rooftop terrace (elevator) and museum costs €25 for adults and €12.50 for children aged 6–18. Children under 6 enter free.

How do I get to Milan Cathedral by metro? +

Take the Milan Metro to Duomo station, served by lines 1 and 3. The Cathedral is right outside the station exit. Tram lines 2, 3, 14 and 24 also stop nearby.

Do I need to book a tour in advance? +

It is strongly recommended to book in advance, especially in peak season. A guided tour includes skip-the-line access and expert commentary on the Cathedral's history and art.
