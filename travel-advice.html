Travel Advice for Milan

Safety, etiquette, useful tips and what to watch out for as a tourist in Milan

✍️ By Ivanka Garcia Mancebo·Updated June 2026

Is Milan safe for tourists?

Milan is a safe city for tourists by any international standard. Violent crime is rare. The main concerns are petty theft and pickpocketing — particularly in crowded tourist areas around the Duomo, on the metro and at Centrale railway station. Keep bags close to your body, be aware in crowded spaces, and don't leave valuables in plain sight.

Pickpocketing and scams

  • Around the Duomo, be alert to distraction techniques — someone asking you to sign a petition, offering a free bracelet, or dropping something at your feet while an accomplice picks your pocket.
  • Only use official white taxis — unlicensed drivers at airports and Centrale can overcharge significantly.
  • If someone offers to "help" you at a ticket machine without being asked, decline politely — they may attempt to steal your card details or demand payment.

Dress codes for churches

All churches in Milan require visitors to have shoulders and knees covered. This applies to the Duomo, Sant'Ambrogio, San Maurizio al Monastero Maggiore and all other religious sites. Carry a scarf or light layer if visiting in summer — you will not be admitted without appropriate coverage.

Drinking water

Tap water in Milan is perfectly safe to drink and of good quality — Milan's water supply comes from Alpine springs and undergoes rigorous testing. There are also public drinking fountains (fontanelle) throughout the city, particularly in parks. Buying bottled water is unnecessary.

Pharmacies

Pharmacies (farmacie) in Milan are identifiable by a green cross sign. They dispense many medications without prescription and are staffed by qualified pharmacists who can advise on minor ailments. Each city area has a duty pharmacy (farmacia di turno) open outside normal hours — the roster is displayed on all pharmacy windows.

Common scams and how to avoid them

Milan is a safe city, but certain scams target tourists specifically. Around the Duomo, the most common involve distraction techniques: someone approaches with a petition or clipboard for you to sign, a "free" bracelet that turns out not to be free, or drops something at your feet while an accomplice picks your pocket. The rule is simple: if a stranger approaches you uninvited in a tourist area, politely decline and walk on.

At Malpensa and Bergamo airports, unlicensed taxi drivers approach arriving passengers in the arrivals hall. They may dress professionally and hold signs with passenger names — but they are not official taxis and charge whatever they wish. Always go to the official taxi rank outside the arrivals exit, or use a pre-booked transfer.

At metro station ticket machines, particularly at Centrale, some individuals offer to "help" you buy a ticket without being asked. Decline politely — they may attempt to steal your card details or demand payment for assistance that was never requested.

Health and medical care

Milan has excellent medical facilities. In a medical emergency, call 118 for an ambulance. The main public hospitals with 24-hour emergency departments (Pronto Soccorso) are Policlinico di Milano (Via Francesco Sforza, 35) and Ospedale Niguarda (Piazza Ospedale Maggiore, 3). EU citizens should carry their EHIC card; UK citizens their GHIC card. Non-EU visitors should have comprehensive travel insurance.

Pharmacies (farmacie) — identified by a green cross — can advise on minor ailments and dispense many medications without a prescription. A duty pharmacy (farmacia di turno) is always open in each area, even on Sundays and public holidays; the current rota is displayed on all pharmacy windows.

Local laws and customs worth knowing

It is illegal to sit on the steps of the Duomo or eat and drink in the immediate vicinity of certain historic monuments — fines are issued. In churches, shoulders and knees must be covered without exception; visitors will be refused entry or given a disposable wrap to cover themselves. Most churches ask for silence and appropriate behaviour during services, which continue even when tourists are present.

Smoking is prohibited in all enclosed public spaces, on public transport and on the platforms of all stations. It is also prohibited in outdoor areas within 10 metres of playgrounds and schools. Fines are actively enforced.

The Italian concept of bella figura — making a good impression — means that Milanese people tend to dress more formally than northern Europeans in public, especially in the evening. While there is no strict dress code in restaurants (except the very formal), being smartly dressed will always be appreciated.

Money and ATMs

The most cost-effective way to access cash in Milan is to withdraw from a bank ATM (Bancomat) using your debit card — avoid the standalone ATMs in tourist areas which charge high fees. Inform your bank before travelling if you are using a card internationally. Most restaurants, shops and museums accept contactless payment; smaller establishments and some markets may still prefer cash.

Frequently asked questions

Is Milan safe for tourists?+

Yes — Milan is a safe city for tourists by any international standard. Violent crime against tourists is rare. The main concern is petty theft and pickpocketing in crowded tourist areas (around the Duomo and on the metro). Be aware of your surroundings, keep bags close to your body, and be alert to distraction techniques used by pickpockets.

Do I need to cover up in Milan churches?+

Yes — all churches in Milan require visitors to have shoulders and knees covered. This applies to the Duomo, Sant'Ambrogio, San Maurizio and all other religious sites. In summer, carry a light scarf or layer. You will not be admitted without appropriate coverage, and no exceptions are made.

Can I drink the tap water in Milan?+

Yes — tap water in Milan is perfectly safe to drink and of excellent quality. Milan's water supply comes from Alpine springs and is rigorously tested. There are also free public drinking fountains (fontanelle) throughout the city. Buying bottled water is unnecessary.

What should I do if I'm pickpocketed in Milan?+

Report the theft to the nearest police station (Questura) as soon as possible — you will need a police report (denuncia) for insurance purposes. In emergencies, call 113 (Italian police) or 112 (EU emergency). Your nearest consulate or embassy can assist with emergency travel documents if your passport is stolen.

Are taxis safe in Milan?+

Official white taxis with a meter and a taxi sign on the roof are safe and regulated. Only use official taxis from designated ranks or booked via the iTaxi app. Never accept rides from drivers who approach you unsolicited at airports or train stations — these are unlicensed and unregulated.
