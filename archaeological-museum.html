Milan Archaeological Museum

The Milan Archaeological Museum (Museo Civico Archeologico di Milano) is housed in the former convent of San Maurizio on Corso Magenta — a building of extraordinary historical layering that sits on top of what was once a 1st-century Roman residential district, the circus maximus and part of the ancient city wall. Collections span Roman Mediolanum, Greek and Etruscan civilisations.

✍️ By Ivanka Garcia Mancebo·Updated June 2026·⏱️ 3 min read

About the museum

The museum occupies the former Benedictine convent of San Maurizio al Monastero Maggiore — the same complex that contains the famous frescoed church next door. The convent's cloister and buildings date from the 16th century, but the ground beneath them tells a much older story: this was a densely inhabited quarter of Roman Milan (Mediolanum), and the remains visible here date back to the 1st and 3rd centuries AD.

The museum is particularly strong on Roman Mediolanum — a subject rarely covered in depth elsewhere in the city. Milan was one of the most important cities of the Roman Empire, briefly serving as the western imperial capital in the late 3rd century, and the collections here reflect that significance.

What to see

The collections are organised by civilisation and period. The first and most impressive section is dedicated to ancient Mediolanum — Roman Milan from the 1st century BC to the 5th century AD. After walking through the rooms, visitors enter the cloister, where they can observe the most remarkable feature of the entire museum: the visible remains of the 3rd-century Roman city wall and a tower, still standing to a substantial height.

The remaining sections cover Greek and Etruscan collections, with ceramics, bronzes and sculptures spanning the classical Mediterranean world. The prehistoric and Egyptian collections have been moved to the Castello Sforzesco.

🏛️ Roman Milan

At its height in the late 3rd and early 4th century AD, Milan (Mediolanum) was arguably more important than Rome itself — it served as the seat of the western Roman Emperor and was the city from which Constantine issued the Edict of Milan in 313 AD, legalising Christianity across the empire. The Archaeological Museum is the best place in Milan to understand this often-overlooked chapter of history.

Visiting tips

  • Closed on Mondays, 1 January, 1 May and 25 December.
  • Free entry on the first Sunday of the month and on the third Tuesday of the month after 2pm.
  • The Roman city wall in the cloister is the highlight — take time to walk around it and read the explanatory panels.
  • Combine with the adjacent San Maurizio al Monastero Maggiore (32 metres), which is also free — both together make a perfect morning on Corso Magenta.
  • The museum is best suited for visitors with an interest in Roman history — for general visitors, the city wall is the unmissable element.

Frequently asked questions

How much does it cost to visit?+

Adults €5, students and seniors over 65 €3, children under 18 free. Free entry on the first Sunday of the month and on the third Tuesday of the month after 2pm.

What are the opening hours?+

Tuesday to Sunday from 10am to 5:30pm. Closed on Mondays, 1 January, 1 May and 25 December.

What can you see in the Milan Archaeological Museum?+

The museum contains Roman finds from ancient Mediolanum (Milan), as well as Greek and Etruscan collections. The most impressive feature is the visible remains of the 3rd-century Roman city wall and tower, which can be seen in the cloister.

How do I get to the Milan Archaeological Museum?+

By metro: Cadorna (lines M1 and M2). By tram: lines 16, 19 and 20. The museum is at Corso Magenta, 15, next door to San Maurizio al Monastero Maggiore.
