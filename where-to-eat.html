Where to Eat in Milan

Milanese cuisine, aperitivo culture & the best neighbourhoods for food

Milan is not only Italy's fashion capital — it is also one of the country's most exciting cities for food. The traditional Milanese kitchen is rich and distinctive, and the city's restaurant scene ranges from neighbourhood trattorias to some of Italy's most creative contemporary kitchens. Plus, Milan invented aperitivo — possibly Italy's finest contribution to civilised life.

✍️ By Ivanka Garcia Mancebo·Updated June 2026

What to eat in Milan — the classics

🌾 Risotto alla Milanese
Saffron-infused risotto — Milan's signature dish. The deep gold colour comes from the saffron; the creaminess from butter and Parmesan added at the end. Traditionally served as an accompaniment to ossobuco, but also served alone.
🥩 Cotoletta alla Milanese
Breaded veal cutlet fried in butter — the original dish that inspired the Wiener Schnitzel. The Milanese version is on the bone and thicker than the Viennese version. Non-negotiable on any visit.
🥧 Panzerotti
Fried dough pockets filled with mozzarella and tomato — Milan's street food at its finest. The best come from Luini (near the Duomo), which has a queue outside virtually every lunchtime.
🥂 Aperitivo
Pre-dinner drinks (Campari Spritz, Negroni, Aperol Spritz) served with complimentary snacks — often so generous they replace dinner. Typically from 6–9pm. Best areas: Navigli, Isola, Brera.

Best areas for eating out

🚢 Navigli
The liveliest area in Milan for evening dining and aperitivo — canal-side restaurants and bars stretching along the Naviglio Grande. Best visited Thursday–Saturday evenings.
🏛️ Brera
Elegant neighbourhood with excellent mid-range and upmarket restaurants, wine bars and cafés. Best for a more refined dining experience in a beautiful setting.
🌿 Isola
Trendy neighbourhood north of Garibaldi — Milan's most interesting area for creative cooking, natural wine bars and concept restaurants. Less touristy than Navigli or Brera.
🍕 Porta Ticinese
Good balance of price and quality. A mix of traditional trattorias and more modern restaurants, popular with students and young Milanese. Close to Navigli but quieter.
💡 Eating well without overspending

The tourist trap zone is the immediate area around the Duomo and Via Dante — restaurants here charge a premium for a mediocre experience. Walk 10 minutes in any direction and the quality improves dramatically while prices drop. A set lunch menu (menù del giorno) at a neighbourhood trattoria — typically €12–€18 for two courses and wine — is Milan's best-value meal.

Meal times in Milan

Lunch is typically served from 12:30pm to 2:30pm. Dinner starts later than in Northern Europe — most Milanese don't sit down until 8pm or later, and kitchens in many restaurants close at 10:30pm or 11pm. Aperitivo is from approximately 6pm to 9pm.

Most restaurants are closed for at least one day per week — typically Sunday or Monday. Always check before visiting.

Frequently asked questions

What is the typical food of Milan?+

Milan's signature dishes are risotto alla milanese (saffron risotto), cotoletta alla milanese (breaded veal cutlet), ossobuco (braised veal shin) and panzerotti (fried dough pockets with mozzarella and tomato — the classic street food).

What is aperitivo in Milan?+

Aperitivo is a pre-dinner drink (Campari Spritz, Negroni, Aperol Spritz) served with complimentary snacks — often so generous they replace dinner. Typically 6–9pm. Navigli and Isola are the best areas.

Where is the best area to eat in Milan?+

Navigli for aperitivo and evening dining, Brera for upmarket restaurants, Isola for creative cooking, Porta Ticinese for good value. Avoid the immediate Duomo area for anything other than coffee.
